        <div class="tile bg-rose-600 text-white rounded-lg shadow-lg mb-6">
            <div class="tile-header p-6 flex justify-between items-center" tabindex="0" role="button" aria-expanded="false" aria-controls="content-subjuntivo">
                <h2 class="text-2xl font-semibold">Present Subjunctive</h2>
                <span class="text-2xl transform transition-transform duration-300 ease-in-out arrow-indicator">+</span>
            </div>
            <div id="content-subjuntivo" class="content-area bg-white text-gray-800 p-6 border-t border-rose-700">
                <p class="mb-4">The Present Subjunctive (Presente de Subjuntivo) is a mood used to express subjectivity, uncertainty, or non-factual situations. Unlike the indicative mood, which states facts, the subjunctive presents actions or states from a personal perspective, often involving desires, doubts, emotions, recommendations, or hypothetical scenarios. It's crucial to understand its triggers, as its use depends heavily on the context provided by the main clause of a sentence or by specific expressions.</p>
                
                <div class="subjunctive-rules mt-4">
                    <h4 class="font-semibold mb-3 text-xl text-rose-700">Key Triggers for the Present Subjunctive:</h4>
                    
                    <h5>1. Wishes, Desires, Commands, and Influence (WEIRDO - W)</h5>
                    <p class="mb-2 text-sm text-gray-600">When the main clause expresses a wish, desire, command, suggestion, permission, prohibition, or any attempt to influence the action of another subject in the subordinate clause (introduced by "que").</p>
                    <ul>
                        <li><strong>Querer que:</strong> Yo quiero que tú <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">hables</span>. (I want you to speak.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Desear que:</strong> Deseo que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">tengas</span> un buen día. (I wish you have a good day.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Pedir que:</strong> Te pido que me <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">ayudes</span>. (I ask you to help me.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Sugerir que:</strong> Sugiero que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">vayamos</span> al cine. (I suggest we go to the movies.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Insistir en que:</strong> Insiste en que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">lleguemos</span> temprano. (He insists that we arrive early.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Prohibir que:</strong> Prohíben que se <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">fume</span> aquí. (They prohibit smoking here.)</li>
                    </ul>

                    <h5>2. Emotions and Feelings (WEIRDO - E)</h5>
                    <p class="mb-2 text-sm text-gray-600">When the main clause expresses an emotional reaction to the action or state in the subordinate clause (introduced by "que").</p>
                    <ul>
                        <li><strong>Alegrarse de que:</strong> Me alegro de que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">estés</span> aquí. (I'm happy that you are here.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Sentir que:</strong> Siento que no <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">puedas</span> venir. (I'm sorry that you can't come.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Temer que / Tener miedo de que:</strong> Temo que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">llueva</span>. (I'm afraid it might rain.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Sorprenderse de que:</strong> Me sorprende que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">sepa</span> tanto. (It surprises me that he knows so much.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Gustar que:</strong> Me gusta que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">sonrías</span>. (I like that you smile.)</li>
                    </ul>

                    <h5>3. Impersonal Expressions (WEIRDO - I)</h5>
                    <p class="mb-2 text-sm text-gray-600">When an impersonal expression in the main clause indicates subjectivity, necessity, possibility, or judgment about the action in the subordinate clause (introduced by "que"). If the impersonal expression states a fact or certainty (e.g., "es verdad que," "es cierto que"), the indicative is used instead.</p>
                    <ul>
                        <li><strong>Es importante que:</strong> Es importante que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">estudies</span>. (It's important that you study.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Es necesario que:</strong> Es necesario que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">durmamos</span> bien. (It's necessary that we sleep well.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Es posible que:</strong> Es posible que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">venga</span> mañana. (It's possible that he/she comes tomorrow.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Es una lástima que:</strong> Es una lástima que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">pierdan</span> el juego. (It's a shame that they lose the game.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Es mejor que:</strong> Es mejor que te <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">quedes</span> en casa. (It's better that you stay home.)</li>
                    </ul>

                    <h5>4. Recommendations and Requests (WEIRDO - R)</h5>
                     <p class="mb-2 text-sm text-gray-600">Similar to influence, when the main clause makes a recommendation, suggestion, or formal request.</p>
                    <ul>
                        <li><strong>Recomendar que:</strong> Te recomiendo que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">leas</span> este libro. (I recommend that you read this book.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Aconsejar que:</strong> Me aconseja que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">descanse</span>. (He/She advises me to rest.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Sugerir que:</strong> (Already covered in 'Wishes')</li>
                    </ul>

                    <h5>5. Doubt, Denial, and Uncertainty (WEIRDO - D)</h5>
                    <p class="mb-2 text-sm text-gray-600">When the main clause expresses doubt, denial, or uncertainty about the truth or reality of the action in the subordinate clause (introduced by "que"). If the main clause expresses certainty or belief (e.g., "creo que," "pienso que," "no dudo que"), the indicative is used.</p>
                    <ul>
                        <li><strong>Dudar que:</strong> Dudo que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">llegue</span> a tiempo. (I doubt he/she will arrive on time.)</li>
                        <li><strong>No creer que:</strong> No creo que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">sea</span> verdad. (I don't believe it's true.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Negar que:</strong> Niega que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">tenga</span> el dinero. (He denies having the money.)</li>
                        <li><strong>No estar seguro/a de que:</strong> No estoy seguro de que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">podamos</span> ir. (I'm not sure we can go.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Quizás / Tal vez (often with subjunctive):</strong> Quizás <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">venga</span>. (Maybe he/she will come.) - Subjunctive implies more doubt.</li>
                    </ul>

                    <h5>6. Ojalá (que) (WEIRDO - O)</h5>
                    <p class="mb-2 text-sm text-gray-600">The expression "Ojalá (que)" always triggers the subjunctive and expresses a strong wish or hope.</p>
                    <ul>
                        <li><strong>Ojalá que:</strong> ¡Ojalá que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">ganemos</span>! (Hopefully, we win! / I really hope we win!)</li>
                        <li><strong>Ojalá:</strong> ¡Ojalá <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">haga</span> buen tiempo! (I hope the weather is good!)</li>
                    </ul>
                    
                    <h5>7. Certain Conjunctions</h5>
                    <p class="mb-2 text-sm text-gray-600">Some conjunctions require the subjunctive when the action in the subordinate clause is anticipated, hypothetical, uncertain, or dependent on a condition. If the action is habitual or a known fact, the indicative is often used with some of these conjunctions (like 'cuando').</p>
                    <ul>
                        <li><strong>Para que (so that, in order that):</strong> Estudio para que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">pueda</span> pasar el examen. (I study so that I can pass the exam.)</li>
                        <li><strong>A menos que (unless):</strong> No saldremos a menos que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">deje</span> de llover. (We won't go out unless it stops raining.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Con tal (de) que (provided that, as long as):</strong> Te presto el coche con tal de que lo <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">cuides</span>. (I'll lend you the car provided that you take care of it.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Sin que (without):</strong> Entró sin que nadie lo <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">viera</span>. (He entered without anyone seeing him.) (Note: This example uses imperfect subjunctive, but the trigger is the same for present if context fits). For present: Hago la tarea sin que mis padres me lo <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">pidan</span>. (I do homework without my parents asking me to.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Antes de que (before):</strong> Llámame antes de que <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">salgas</span>. (Call me before you leave.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Cuando (when - referring to a future, uncompleted action):</strong> Cuando <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">llegues</span>, avísame. (When you arrive, let me know.) Compare with indicative: Cuando llego a casa, descanso. (When I arrive home [habitually], I rest.)</li>
                        <li><strong>Aunque (although, even if - expressing a hypothetical or uncertain situation):</strong> Aunque <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">llueva</span>, iré. (Even if it rains, I will go.) Compare with indicative: Aunque llueve, voy. (Although it is raining [fact], I am going.)</li>
                    </ul>
                     <p class="mt-3 mb-4 text-sm text-gray-700"><strong>Key Condition: Change of Subject.</strong> For many of these triggers (especially wishes, emotions, impersonal expressions, doubt), the subjunctive is used when there is a change of subject between the main clause and the subordinate clause (connected by "que"). If the subject is the same, an infinitive is typically used instead of "que" + subjunctive. Ex: <span class="italic">Quiero <span class="font-bold">viajar</span></span> (I want to travel) vs. <span class="italic">Quiero que tú <span class="font-bold text-rose-500">viajes</span></span> (I want you to travel).</p>
                </div>
