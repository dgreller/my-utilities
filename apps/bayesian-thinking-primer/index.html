What is Bayesian Thinking?

Bayesian thinking is a way of updating our beliefs in light of new evidence. Instead of thinking in terms of absolute certainty, we think in terms of probabilities. As we gather more evidence, we update our probabilities to get closer to the truth.

The core of Bayesian thinking is Bayes' Theorem:

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)
  • P(A|B) (Posterior): The probability of our hypothesis (A) being true, given the new evidence (B).
  • P(B|A) (Likelihood): The probability of observing the evidence (B), assuming our hypothesis (A) is true.
  • P(A) (Prior): The initial probability of our hypothesis (A) being true, before we see the new evidence.
  • P(B) (Marginal): The total probability of observing the evidence (B).

A Practical Example: Medical Diagnosis

Imagine a rare disease that affects 1% of the population. There is a test for this disease that is 99% accurate (i.e., if you have the disease, it will be positive 99% of the time, and if you don't have the disease, it will be negative 99% of the time).

You test positive. What is the probability that you actually have the disease?

  • P(A) (Prior): The probability of having the disease = 0.01
  • P(B|A) (Likelihood): The probability of testing positive if you have the disease = 0.99
  • P(B) (Marginal): The total probability of testing positive. This is the sum of true positives and false positives: (0.99 * 0.01) + (0.01 * 0.99) = 0.0198
